<p>Héctor Rodríguez, the 22-year-old Reds outfield prospect acquired in the 2022 Tyler Naquin trade, has been one of the most aggressive hitters in the minors for four years. In 2025, he swung at 60 percent of all pitches (97.5th percentile in AAA) and chased 47 percent of pitches outside the zone (worse than 97.3 percent of the league). His <span class="term" data-term="bb% (batter)">walk rate</span> was 5.2 percent. In 2026, across 128 Dominican Winter League plate appearances and 98 more at Triple-A Louisville, the approach has changed. His walk rate has tripled to 16.3 percent, his <span class="term" data-term="k% (batter)">strikeout rate</span> barely moved (16.5% to 17.3%), and his <span class="term" data-term="woba">wOBA</span> jumped from .307 to .394. Every projection system still models the old version of him. The Reds' coaching staff says the change was deliberate.</p>

<p style="margin-top:1rem;"><strong>If you're new to baseball:</strong> Walk rate measures how often a hitter takes four balls and earns a free base instead of swinging. A hitter who swings at everything, even bad pitches, almost never walks, which means he's constantly making outs on pitches he had no chance of hitting well. Rodríguez used to be that guy. The data says he's learning which pitches to leave alone, and the pitches he does swing at are producing much better results.</p>

<p style="margin-top:1rem;"><strong>If you manage a fantasy team:</strong> He's not on your radar yet, and he shouldn't be until he reaches the majors. But the Reds' outfield situation (TJ Friedl's zero extra-base hits, Rece Hinds struggling after his call-up, Noelvi Marte optioned) is opening a path. Rodríguez is on the 40-man roster. If he gets the call, watch his walk rate through 60 <span class="term" data-term="pa">plate appearances</span>: above 10 percent means the approach change survived the jump and he's a speculative add in 12-team leagues and deeper. Below 7 percent means the old habits returned.</p>

</div>
</details>

For four years, professional evaluators who watched Héctor Rodríguez kept writing the same scouting report. *Baseball America* wrote in their 2025 evaluation that "if the ball doesn't bounce before the plate, Rodriguez may swing at it." FanGraphs flagged his "48% chase rate" as "the third highest in the minors among hitters with at least 400 PA" and warned of "substantial bust risk."

Every report identified the same limitation: one of the best bats in the minors, one of the worst approaches. Rodríguez's hand-eye coordination let him barrel pitches six inches off the plate, but that aggression also meant he was chasing nearly half the pitches thrown outside the zone. In High-A in 2024, his <span class="term" data-term="bb% (batter)">walk rate</span> was a tiny 4.8 percent, and a 13.3 percent strikeout rate was the only reason it didn't sink him. That can work against High-A pitchers. It won't work in the big leagues.

And yet nobody could quite give up on him. An unnamed observer at Reds spring training told *The Athletic*'s C. Trent Rosecrans that Rodríguez would "swing at the rosin bag, but he'll hit it."

Keith Law, while ranking him fifth in the Reds system in February, probably said it best. To him, Rodríguez looked like "a 20- to 25-homer, low-OBP corner outfielder, which is at least an everyday guy, and maybe more with some modest changes to his approach that focus more on swinging at pitches he can hit hard."

I pulled his 2026 numbers expecting to find more of the same. Instead, the data from the last five months says those modest changes may be happening, and they aren't modest at all.

If you've been following Exit Velo's coverage of the Reds' outfield situation, you know the context. [TJ Friedl has been struggling at the plate](/articles/whats-eating-tj-friedl.html) (he hit doubles in back-to-back games immediately after I wrote about his extra-base drought, because baseball is like that). [Rece Hinds was called up](/articles/rece-hinds-different-hitter.html) after Noelvi Marte was optioned. He isn't hitting much, but the hits he's gotten have been timely. Despite these glimmers of hope, the Reds still need another solid outfield option. Rodríguez, who is on the 40-man roster and has been the best hitter in Louisville, is the obvious next name in that conversation.

Who remembers who the Reds got for Tyler Naquin at the 2022 trade deadline? Two 18-year-olds from the Mets' rookie ball team. One was right-hander José Acuña. The other was a compact, left-handed hitting second baseman from Santo Domingo named Héctor Junior Rodríguez, who didn't appear on any top prospect lists at the time. FanGraphs ranked him 49th in the Reds system after the deal, gave him a 35+ <span class="term" data-term="future value">future value</span> grade, and described him as "a talented developmental utility prospect in the very early stages of his pro career." His power was graded at 20 on the <span class="term" data-term="20-80 scale">20-to-80 scale</span>, which is about as low as evaluators go.

Despite FanGraphs' rather tepid assessment of the young Rodríguez, he was battling for the Florida Complex League batting title when the trade happened. He hit .356/.387/.558 for the Mets' complex team before the deal, then kept hitting after switching organizations. He has never hit below .280 in a professional season.

What happened over the next three years reshaped everything about the player except the one thing scouts kept circling back to. He dropped switch-hitting to focus exclusively on the left side. He moved from second base to center field to corner outfield as he got stronger and slower, his speed grade falling from 70 to 50 across three evaluations. His power climbed from a 30 grade to 45 as he filled out to 5-foot-10, 200 pounds. *Baseball America* graded his <span class="term" data-term="hit tool">hit tool</span> between 55 and 60 every year, the kind of bat-to-ball skill that scouts rarely argue about.

He won the Dominican Winter League Rookie of the Year award in 2023-24, hitting .309 with an .831 <span class="term" data-term="ops">OPS</span> at 19 years old on a team where the average position player was 27 and the only other teenager was Junior Caminero, now a Tampa Bay All-Star. His team won the DWL title the following winter under manager Albert Pujols. He struck out 16.5 percent of the time in Triple-A in 2025, and *The Athletic* reported that only 30 qualified big leaguers posted a better strikeout rate that season. None of them were on the Reds.

Nick Krall told *The Athletic* in January that Rodríguez "can absolutely help the Reds in a big way in 2026." Terry Francona said, "He can hit. He can flat out hit." Brad Meador, the GM who acquired him, said something more measured that I think captures the deal perfectly. "You try to pick the guys you like, but when they're that young there's still so much development. You never know."

They picked the right guy. But the approach problem followed him at every level. His ranking in the Reds' system climbed from 49th to fifth across four evaluations, and every single one listed the same limitation. FanGraphs' December 2025 report, which gave him a 45 future value, put it plainly: "This guy swings at everything... there's a pretty good chance that he ends up a sub-.300 OBP hitter." The future value jumped despite the concern, not because of any change in the approach. The bat was that good.

The evaluators were right, and the <span class="term" data-term="statcast">Statcast</span> data from 2025 confirmed everything they were seeing with their eyes.

In 230 plate appearances at Triple-A Louisville last season, Rodríguez swung at 60.1 percent of all pitches. That ranked in the 97.5th percentile among AAA hitters, meaning only 2.5 percent of the league swung more often than he did. His <span class="term" data-term="chase rate (batter)">chase rate</span>, the percentage of pitches outside the strike zone that he offered at, was 47.0 percent. That ranked in the 2.7th percentile, meaning 97.3 percent of AAA hitters showed better pitch selection.

His walk rate was 5.2 percent. His wOBA and <span class="term" data-term="xwoba">xwOBA</span> were both .307. Roughly league average at Triple-A, from a hitter whose raw tools were anything but.

Savant's prospect model rated those tools in the 91.2nd percentile. His strikeout rate was 16.5 percent, which ranked in the 81.6th percentile. His max <span class="term" data-term="exit velocity">exit velocity</span> of 112.7 mph sat in the 89th percentile. The physical tools were excellent. The approach was burying them.

The evaluators were not wrong. They were accurately describing the player who existed. The scouting report wrote itself because the data confirmed it every year, independently, across multiple platforms. FanGraphs saw it. BA saw it. Savant's percentile rankings saw it. The problem was real, and it limited his production at every level.

The Rece Hinds anecdote from *The Athletic* captures the issue perfectly. Last summer, Rodríguez was taking an at-bat in Triple-A. He fouled off pitch after pitch after pitch. On the 10th pitch, he smoked a 108 mph line drive up the middle for a base hit. Hinds checked the iPad in the dugout afterward. None of the 10 pitches were in the strike zone.

"The fact that he can make hard contact on the barrel so consistently is an incredible thing," Hinds said. That's the tool. The 10-pitch at-bat on pitches he shouldn't have been swinging at is the problem. The gap between what his contact quality deserved and what his approach allowed him to produce was the scouting report, written on every evaluation, for four years.

The change didn't start in April. It started in winter ball.

In the 2025-26 Dominican Winter League, Rodríguez hit .301/.375/.504 with four home runs in 128 <span class="term" data-term="pa">plate appearances</span> for Escogido. He walked 13 times and struck out 13 times. That's an even strikeout-to-walk ratio, something he had never come close to in any previous professional season.

Reds hitting coach Chris Valaika told *The Athletic* in January that "something with him has been controlling the strike zone as a priority." He added that "what's impressed him the most is that Rodríguez has an even strikeout-to-walk ratio" in winter ball.

This matters because it establishes the timeline. The Reds' coaching staff identified the approach as the priority. Rodríguez worked on it in winter ball, where the competition level is high but the stakes allow experimentation. The .375 OBP in the DWL wasn't a fluke month. It was 128 plate appearances against a roster stocked with current and former big leaguers, including the team he'd already won a title with under Albert Pujols.

Then the regular season started, and the numbers in Louisville picked up where winter ball left off.

The strikeout rate barely moving is the most important row in that table. He isn't walking more because he's become passive. He isn't taking strikes he used to swing at. He's still making contact at the same rate. He's choosing better pitches to swing at. The <span class="term" data-term="swstr%">swinging strike rate</span> dropped from 13.3 percent to 8.6 percent across 370 pitches in 2026. He's not just swinging less. He's swinging and *missing* less on the pitches he does offer at. That's selectivity, not passivity.

*Sports Illustrated* reported on April 20 that his swing rate has dropped from 60 percent to roughly 40 percent. I can't independently verify that number from our FanGraphs data (the MiLB exports don't include swing rate), but the FanGraphs aggregates support the direction: you don't triple your walk rate and cut your swinging strike rate by nearly five points without a substantial drop in overall swing frequency.

The combined sample across winter ball and Louisville is now 226 plate appearances showing the same directional change. Walk rate stabilizes at around 120 plate appearances, and these 226 span two different environments: Dominican Winter League and Triple-A. That's more than a hot month, but it's not 120 PA of MLB data.

Stewart plays in the majors and Rodríguez plays in AAA, so this isn't an apples-to-apples comparison. But Sal Stewart, who has played alongside Rodríguez since rookie ball and is now posting a 161 <span class="term" data-term="wrc+">wRC+</span> at the major league level, told *The Athletic* that "his bat speed is through the roof. He can hit for power, hit for average, hit balls inside the zone or outside the zone. It's really impressive. He's a good buddy of mine. People better watch out when he comes through." Stewart bats right-handed, and Rodríguez left-handed, so the platoon context is different too. The comparison isn't about level of competition or handedness. It's about the rate of change: Rodríguez's current walk rate (16.3 percent) exceeds Stewart's 2025 AAA rate (11.5 percent), and his strikeout rate (17.3 percent) matches Stewart's current MLB number (18.1 percent). He moved from the 14th percentile in walk rate to numbers that would place him among the most disciplined hitters in the Reds' system at any level. That trajectory is the argument.

So why haven't the projection systems caught up? <span class="term" data-term="projection systems">Projection systems</span> are statistical models that estimate what a player will do for the rest of the season, mostly by averaging his past performance and regressing it toward league norms. I pulled all four expecting them to have updated at least partially. They haven't moved. OOPSY projects a 3.7 percent walk rate. BATX and ATC sit at 4.6 and 4.7 percent. Steamer, the most generous, projects 5.2. His actual rate is 16.3 percent, three to four times what any system expects.

The gap looks damning, but the models are doing exactly what they're designed to do. Projection systems are conservative by nature. They anchor to career norms, weight recent seasons heavily, and update incrementally. For a 22-year-old with a career walk rate below 6 percent, projecting more of the same is the statistically correct default. The models aren't broken. They can't see a behavioral change until the new data accumulates enough to move the anchor.

The gap is still the story. If Rodríguez's walk rate is genuinely different, the projections will catch up by midseason as the 2026 PA pile up and the career-to-date anchor shifts. If it isn't, they'll be vindicated. But right now, 226 plate appearances of approach change exist that the models haven't absorbed yet.

When I first saw the 16.3 percent walk rate, my instinct was to write it off as a hot month. But the timeline complicates that instinct, and so does the sample.

Walk rate stabilizes at roughly 120 plate appearances. Rodríguez has 98 in Louisville and 128 in winter ball, but those are different competitive environments, and the DWL is not the International League. The direction is strong. The magnitude is still in motion. His true-talent walk rate might land at 10 or 11 percent, not 16.3 percent, and that would still represent a meaningful change from his career baseline of 5 percent without vindicating the current pace.

Triple-A pitching isn't major league pitching. I covered this in the Hinds article, and the same caveat applies here. The velocity gap is real, the sequencing is smarter, and the breaking balls are sharper. The discipline shift that works against 89 mph AAA <span class="term" data-term="slider">sliders</span> may not hold against 94 mph MLB ones.

His <span class="term" data-term="iffb% (batter)">infield fly ball rate</span> of 29.2 percent is a concern. The MiLB league average sits around 10 to 12 percent. That means nearly a third of his fly balls are weak popups, which suppresses his power ceiling even at Great American Ball Park, where the three-year <span class="term" data-term="park factor">HR park factor</span> is 119. I don't have a 2025 comparable from the Savant leaderboard data, so I can't say whether this is new or persistent, but it limits the upside of whatever the approach change unlocks.

FanGraphs' December 2025 report described him as having "roughly average <span class="term" data-term="bat speed">bat speed</span>" with "above-average measurable raw power." Stewart told *The Athletic* his "bat speed is through the roof." Both can be true if Stewart is seeing hand quickness and timing that a Statcast bat tracking system measures differently. There is no Statcast bat tracking data for Rodríguez at the MiLB level, so we're left with a FanGraphs scouting assessment and a teammate's eye test pointing in different directions.

There are no platoon splits available at the minor league level. As a left-handed hitter, his performance against left-handed pitching will be critical for a full-time role. That's a genuine unknown.

Observers at Reds camp compared Rodríguez to José Ramírez for his size, Carlos Delgado for his easy power, and Luis Arraez for his bat-to-ball skills. Those are aspirational comps for a 22-year-old who hasn't taken an MLB at-bat, but they speak to the range of tools the organization sees. Valaika described him as "twitchy," comparing his power style to Noelvi Marte's, the kind that produces "110 mph line drives more than 450-foot home runs." If the approach change holds, the floor is probably an everyday left fielder who hits for average with moderate pop. If it doesn't, the FanGraphs projection of a platoon corner outfielder was the right read all along.

I can't tell you this is permanent. I can tell you it's a change, a significant one, visible across two competitive environments and consistent with a deliberate coaching intervention. Whether it's durable at the major league level is a question that 226 plate appearances of minor league data can point toward but not answer. Only MLB pitching can answer it.

FanGraphs' December 2025 report described Rodríguez as a "righty-mashing platoon outfielder" with "a pretty good chance that he ends up a sub-.300 OBP hitter." He's currently posting a .398 OBP. His true talent probably lives somewhere between those two assessments, and even the middle ground, a 10 to 11 percent walk rate with his existing contact skills, would be a different player than the one who appeared on every scouting report for four years.

*Baseball America*'s 2025 evaluation said it most directly. "There remains a worry this approach won't work once pitchers figure it out, and Rodriguez's low walk rate is starting to impact his production." The low walk rate isn't low anymore. Whether the change survives contact with major league pitching is the question that Louisville can't answer and Cincinnati will have to.

I'm not going to pretend 226 plate appearances resolves a question that four years of scouting reports said couldn't be resolved. But the direction is clear, the coaching staff says it's deliberate, and the path to Great American Ball Park is opening up. The Reds need an outfielder. The data says there's one in Louisville who isn't the same hitter he was a year ago.

If Hinds' strikeout rate stays above 40 percent and Friedl's extra-base power doesn't return, the Reds' outfield math points to Rodríguez by late May or June. He's already on the 40-man roster, so the move requires no corresponding transaction.

His walk rate over the next 50 plate appearances will tell us how much of this is real. I'll be watching.
