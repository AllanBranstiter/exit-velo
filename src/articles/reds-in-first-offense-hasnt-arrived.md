<p>The Reds are 16–9 and in first place in the NL Central, but they rank as the unluckiest offense in baseball by the gap between their <span class="term" data-term="woba">wOBA</span> and <span class="term" data-term="xwoba">xwOBA</span>, a comparison that isolates who is genuinely slumping versus who is hitting rockets at defenders. Two players account for most of that gap: Ke'Bryan Hayes owns the single largest luck deficit among all 275 qualified hitters in MLB, with a .077 <span class="term" data-term="babip (batter)">BABIP</span> despite elite <span class="term" data-term="contact rate">contact rates</span> and improved discipline, and Tyler Stephenson is hitting the ball nearly 5 mph harder than last season while batting .158 with a .194 BABIP. But the luck narrative doesn't cover the whole roster. TJ Friedl's collapse is mostly real, not bad fortune. Matt McLain's swing changes haven't translated into results. And Eugenio Suárez is actually getting <em>lucky</em>, not unlucky. The honest picture is a first-place team with two hitters getting historically punished by batted ball variance, a few hitters with genuine performance problems, and a couple of guys, Elly De La Cruz and Sal Stewart, performing exactly as well as they should be. The pitching and defense are already carrying. When the offense catches up, this team has another level.</p>

<p style="margin-top:1rem;"><strong>If you're new to baseball:</strong> When a hitter makes solid contact but the ball goes right where a fielder is standing, that's bad luck. <span class="term" data-term="xwoba">Expected wOBA</span> strips out where the ball landed and estimates what <em>should</em> have happened based on how hard and at what angle the ball was hit. If expected wOBA is much higher than actual wOBA, the hitter's quality of contact is better than his results. That's what's happening to a few Reds right now.</p>

<p style="margin-top:1rem;"><strong>The short version:</strong> The Reds are winning with the pitching staff and defense. Hayes and Stephenson are due for major corrections that should make the offense significantly better. Friedl's problems are real and go deeper than bad luck. Stewart and De La Cruz are already producing. This team has offensive upside it hasn't used yet.</p>

.077.

That's not a slugging percentage. It's not a walk rate. It's a <span class="term" data-term="babip (batter)">batting average on balls in play</span> for a hitter who's squaring the ball up on 46 percent of his competitive swings, striking out less than 10 percent of the time, and making contact on 81 percent of all pitches he offers at.

It belongs to Ke'Bryan Hayes, through 65 plate appearances and 53 batted ball events. Among all 275 hitters with 50 or more plate appearances, it's the worst.

I've been staring at the Reds' early-season numbers trying to reconcile two realities. The team is 16–9, in first place in the NL Central, winning games on pitching and defense while the bats figure things out. And yet Cincinnati ranks as the unluckiest offense in baseball by PA-weighted <span class="term" data-term="xwoba">xwOBA</span> minus <span class="term" data-term="woba">wOBA</span> gap. The team's actual offensive production (.299 wOBA) lags 28 points behind what the quality of their contact deserves (.327 xwOBA). No other team in baseball has a wider gap. They're in first place and the offense is still in the chamber.

But "the Reds are just unlucky" is too simple, and it obscures what's actually happening across this lineup. Some of these hitters are being robbed. Some of them are genuinely struggling. And telling them apart matters, because a first-place team with offensive upside still to come looks different than a first-place team that's already at capacity.

I went through the lineup player by player. The story broke into three parts: two hitters experiencing historically awful batted ball fortune, a few hitters with genuine problems that no amount of BABIP correction will fix, and a couple of guys performing exactly to expectation and getting overlooked because of the noise around them.

## Hayes and Stephenson

Hayes is the headline, and it isn't close. His .085 batting average and .154 wOBA look like a player who forgot how to hit. But every underlying metric disagrees. His <span class="term" data-term="exit velocity">exit velocity</span> is 90.2 mph, respectable for a player who's never been a power hitter. His <span class="term" data-term="k% (batter)">strikeout rate</span> dropped from 19.5 percent last year in Pittsburgh to 9.2 percent in Cincinnati. His <span class="term" data-term="bb% (batter)">walk rate</span> improved from 6.3 to 9.2 percent. His <span class="term" data-term="swstr%">swinging strike rate</span> is 7.5 percent, which is outstanding. He is making more contact, better contact, and walking more than he has at any point in his career.

And he's batting .085.

The <span class="term" data-term="squared-up rate">squared-up rate</span> is where it gets surreal. Hayes is centering the ball on the bat 46 percent of the time, the highest on the roster by a wide margin. His <span class="term" data-term="blast rate">blast rate</span>, which measures the most violent collisions between bat and ball, jumped from 13.3 percent in 2025 to 17.1 percent. He's putting good wood on pitches more consistently than he ever has. The xwOBA-wOBA gap of +.159 is the largest in all of baseball, not just on the Reds, not just in the NL Central, but among all 275 hitters with enough plate appearances to qualify.

The caveat is that Hayes was never going to be a power threat regardless. His <span class="term" data-term="fast-swing rate">fast-swing rate</span> cratered from 11.8 to 3.9 percent, his <span class="term" data-term="bat speed">bat speed</span> sits at 70.4 mph (below average), and his <span class="term" data-term="max exit velocity">max exit velocity</span> dropped from 109.7 to 104.6. He's squaring the ball up without elite force behind it. When the BABIP corrects — and it will, because .077 doesn't sustain itself for a player making this much contact — you're looking at a .240 hitter with gap power and elite defense. Not a breakout. A functional player getting the worst luck in baseball, and it's not even a contest.

Tyler Stephenson is the case that actually makes me angry, because this isn't just bad luck riding a mediocre performance. He's better than he's ever been, and the box score looks like he's been sent down.

His average exit velocity jumped from 90.5 mph in 2025 to 95.4 in 2026. That's a 4.9 mph gain. His <span class="term" data-term="hard-hit rate (batter)">hard-hit rate</span> went from 49.2 to 55.3 percent. His <span class="term" data-term="chase rate (batter)">chase rate</span> improved from 29.1 to 18.0 percent, which is elite selectivity, the kind of number that usually comes with a 14 percent walk rate, and, oh, his walk rate is 14.7 percent. His K rate actually dropped from 33.9 to 29.4 percent.

Every single underlying metric improved. Every one.

He's batting .158 with a .194 BABIP.

In 68 plate appearances, his <span class="term" data-term="expected batting average">expected batting average</span> is .230, 72 points above his actual .158. His xwOBA of .344 would project a <span class="term" data-term="wrc+">wRC+</span> in the mid-90s if his results caught up to his contact quality. Instead he's posting a 55 wRC+. The gap between what he's doing to the baseball and what the baseball is doing to his stat line is the sixth largest in the sport.

I should note one complication. His <span class="term" data-term="sweet spot rate">sweet spot rate</span> from Savant dropped from 42.2 to 36.8 percent. He's hitting the ball harder but at less optimal angles. And when he does chase, which is rare now, his out-of-zone contact rate has fallen to 29.6 percent, meaning those swings are mostly empty. But at an 18 percent chase rate, that 29.6 percent is built on roughly 27 swings and 8 contacts. I wouldn't build an argument on 8 contacts.

## Friedl, McLain, and Suárez

Now the part that requires honesty.

TJ Friedl is not just unlucky. [I wrote about this earlier this month](/articles/whats-eating-tj-friedl.html), and the numbers haven't changed. His exit velocity is 87.1 mph, identical to 2025, which was already in the bottom third of the league. His sweet spot rate, the percentage of batted balls hit at <span class="term" data-term="launch angle">launch angles</span> that produce line drives and fly balls rather than grounders and pop-ups, collapsed from 32.8 to 20.3 percent. His chase rate spiked from 21.7 to 31.3 percent. He has zero <span class="term" data-term="barrel">barrels</span> in 97 plate appearances, in a park that inflates home runs by 19 percent above league average.

His xwOBA is .243. Even if every batted ball had landed exactly where the Statcast model predicted, he'd still be one of the worst everyday hitters in baseball. The 20-point gap between his expected and actual stats is real, but it's a rounding error on top of a genuine decline. Something changed in Friedl's approach heading into this season. The data suggests it started before April.

Matt McLain is the puzzle I keep coming back to. His bat speed jumped 2 mph, his fast-swing rate nearly tripled, his chase rate dropped 6 points, and his strikeout rate fell from 28.9 to 24.3 percent. These are exactly the kind of mechanical and approach upgrades you'd want to see from a 26-year-old middle infielder.

His exit velocity dropped 2 mph. His hard-hit rate fell from 40.6 to 27.9 percent. He has a zero percent <span class="term" data-term="hr/fb (batter)">home run to fly ball rate</span> on a <span class="term" data-term="fly ball rate (batter)">fly ball rate</span> of 54.1 percent.

He's swinging faster and making less hard contact. I expected the bat speed gains to show up in exit velocity. They haven't. My best guess is timing: he's adjusting to a quicker, more decisive swing and hasn't yet grooved the coordination between the faster bat and the contact point. Spencer Steer's numbers tell a similar but simpler story: his <span class="term" data-term="barrel rate (batter)">barrel rate</span> more than doubled, but his strikeout rate jumped 8 points, and the two are roughly canceling each other out. His max exit velocity is still 110.5 mph, so the raw power is there. The zero HR/FB rate in a 119 <span class="term" data-term="park factor">park factor</span> stadium will correct because at some point he's going to hit one of those 54 percent fly balls over the fence. But the 86.8 mph average exit velocity and the 27.9 percent hard-hit rate aren't going to be solved by luck alone. Those need the timing to catch up to the bat speed.

And then there's Eugenio Suárez, the player nobody's talking about in the luck conversation because the luck conversation is running in the wrong direction. Suárez is hitting .241 with a .314 wOBA. Respectable numbers for a 34-year-old. His xwOBA is .280. His exit velocity is 85.6 mph. His hard-hit rate is 20.7 percent. His contact quality projects a much worse hitter than his stat line currently shows.

He's not unlucky. He's the opposite. His .327 BABIP is inflated, and when it corrects downward, his surface stats are going to get worse, not better. The early-season luck that the Reds haven't gotten from Hayes and Stephenson, Suárez has been quietly absorbing. It partially offsets the team total, masking how extreme the individual gaps for Hayes and Stephenson are.

## De La Cruz and Stewart

The genuinely encouraging part of this roster doesn't need the luck conversation at all. Elly De La Cruz is hitting .276 with a .399 wOBA and a .393 xwOBA. Those numbers are within 6 points of each other, which means his results accurately reflect his contact quality. His exit velocity is 95.5 mph, his barrel rate is 16.4 percent, and his bat speed of 75.0 mph leads the team by a wide margin. De La Cruz is playing to his talent level. That's what it looks like when a 24-year-old switch-hitter with generational athleticism and a 148 wRC+ is doing exactly what the data says he should be doing.

Sal Stewart is the other bright spot, and [I wrote about why his breakout is backed by real structural changes earlier this month](/articles/sal-stewart-isnt-the-next-joey-votto.html). His wOBA (.437) slightly exceeds his xwOBA (.426), meaning he's earned his production through contact quality rather than luck. His 22-year-old season has featured a K rate drop from 25.9 to 17.3 percent, a walk rate that nearly tripled from 5.2 to 14.4 percent, and a barrel rate of 22.5 percent. The approach overhaul is the stickiest kind of improvement because it shows up in the metrics that stabilize fastest at small samples. The discipline changed, and the results followed.

These are sober estimates that weight prior-year data heavily and don't overreact to April. Hayes' .283 projection is the most modest, but for a plus defender, that's a useful player.

I think the projections are roughly right for everyone except Friedl, where I'm less optimistic than the systems, and Stephenson, where I think the underlying improvement is real enough that he could outperform the projection once BABIP corrects. But the broader picture is clear: this is a lineup that should be producing more than it has, and the correction is a matter of when, not if, for the players who are genuinely being robbed.

The numbers I'm watching are specific. Stephenson's exit velocity: if it holds above 93 mph through May, the early-season jump is real and not a 38 batted-ball-event artifact. Hayes' BABIP: anything above .200 means the universe is slowly righting itself. McLain's hard-hit rate: if it climbs back above 35 percent, the bat speed gains are translating. And the team-wide xwOBA-wOBA gap: it's +.028 right now. By June, it should be within .010 of zero. If it isn't, we're talking about something stranger than BABIP variance.

The Reds are 16–9 with the unluckiest offense in baseball. Parts of this lineup are genuinely struggling, and we should be honest about that. But the players carrying the heaviest statistical burdens are also the ones whose underlying quality most clearly contradicts their results. Hayes and Stephenson aren't just unlucky by Reds standards. They're unlucky by the standards of every hitter in baseball. That corrects. It always does. And when it does, a first-place team with this pitching staff and this much offensive upside still in reserve becomes something the rest of the NL Central should be worried about.
