A handful of contact-quality and discipline measurements get there much faster, in about 50 plate appearances. This piece uses those faster-stabilizing metrics to find prospects who are making real adjustments, not just getting lucky.</p>

<p style="margin-top:1rem;"><strong>If you manage a fantasy team:</strong> Watson is the highest-upside play (on Cleveland's 40-man roster). I ran the same <a href="/articles/grading-the-2025-rookie-class.html">Prospect Scorecard</a> I built for the 2025 rookie class on 40 MiLB prospects (age 25 and under, 50-plus <span class="term" data-term="plate appearance">plate appearances</span> in 2026), then added a year-over-year improvement layer comparing each player's 2026 numbers to 2025. Three names stood out. **Kahlil Watson** (CLE) ranked first in composite improvement, with his <span class="term" data-term="o-swing%">chase rate</span> dropping 14 points and four of five scorecard metrics moving in the right direction. **Sam Antonacci** (CHW) has the best plate discipline in the entire pool, a fifth-round pick nobody ranked twelve months ago. And **Esmerlyn Valdez** (PIT) is combining the third-highest <span class="term" data-term="barrel rate (batter)">barrel rate</span> in the pool with a documented mechanical overhaul at age 22.

On the watch list: **Bryce Eldridge** (SFG) is a top-20 overall prospect whose scorecard says his results are worse than his contact quality, which is what you want to see from a 21-year-old recovering from wrist surgery. **Hector Rodriguez** (CIN) posted the largest single-metric discipline improvement in the data set. And **James Tibbs III** (LAD) is the hottest hitter in the minors, but the scorecard sees something the <span class="term" data-term="box score">box score</span> doesn't.

Watson was the 16th overall pick in 2021 out of Wake Forest High School. Miami took him as a tooled-out shortstop, but on-and-off-field issues and a 35 percent <span class="term" data-term="k% (batter)">strikeout rate</span> in Low-A led them to move him in the Josh Bell deal at the 2023 deadline. Cleveland rebuilt him from the ground up, converting him to center field, sending him back to Double-A, and investing three years in a player every scouting report describes as having elite physical tools (<span class="term" data-term="20-80 scale">50/55 raw power, 55/55 run, 70-grade arm</span>) held back by one thing: he chases too much and struggles against spin. FanGraphs gives him a 30/40 <span class="term" data-term="hit tool">hit tool</span>. Baseball America calls his approach "inconsistent." Both project him as a fourth outfielder, contingent on whether the contact ever improves.

I expected the scorecard to confirm that projection. It didn't.

**The early 2026 data says the contact is improving.** His chase rate dropped 14 points year-over-year, the second-largest discipline improvement in the entire pool (behind Hector Rodriguez's 18.3-point drop, discussed below), and his <span class="term" data-term="swstr%">swinging strike rate</span> moved in the same direction. The <span class="term" data-term="luck index">Luck Index</span> flipped from negative to positive, meaning his contact quality now exceeds his surface results. Four of five scorecard metrics moved the right way. Multiple independent metrics all trending the same direction is what makes this signal stronger than any single number at 57 PA. I'm not ready to rewrite the scouting report, but the exact weakness every evaluator flags is the one that's trending in the right direction, and Cleveland's development track record gives me reason to believe the improvement is coached rather than random.

**Verdict:** Stash now in leagues with NA spots. Watson is on Cleveland's 40-man roster, and if the discipline improvement holds even partially, the "fourth outfielder" projection needs to be updated. The number to watch is his chase rate at 120 PA.

## Sam Antonacci, 2B, Chicago White Sox

A year ago, Antonacci was a fifth-round pick out of Coastal Carolina with no ranking on any major prospect list. He now ranks first on the Prospect Scorecard. He played for Team Italy in the World Baseball Classic, and scouts are calling him one of the biggest eye-openers of the spring. FanGraphs gives him a 60/60 hit tool and explicitly comps him to Chase Meidroth, which is the same comp I arrived at independently through the scorecard data before reading their report.

**The discipline numbers are the best in the pool.** He almost never swings and misses, and he almost never chases, ranking first in both swinging strike rate and chase rate among all 40 prospects. That's the kind of floor that translates directly to the majors. The power ceiling is the honest limitation. His Barrel Rate is middling, and FanGraphs grades his game power at just 30/35. But multiple scouting reports from the World Baseball Classic and spring training describe a bigger, stronger player with increased bat speed. His 90th-percentile <span class="term" data-term="exit velocity">exit velocity</span>, the threshold that captures his hardest-hit balls, jumped to 103.1 mph in 2025. The raw power may be arriving.

He also stole 48 bases last year on instincts rather than pure speed, which adds real fantasy value in categories leagues. The Meidroth comp captures the hit tool and the patience, but if the power is ticking upward and you add 40-plus stolen bases, you're looking at a different kind of player than Meidroth was. That's the gap between a useful regular and a fantasy asset with multi-category upside.

**Verdict:** Stash in all leagues with 12-plus teams. The discipline is elite and already past its stabilization threshold at 53 PA. The Meidroth comp sets the floor. The emerging power and stolen base production could raise the ceiling. Chicago has every incentive to give him big league reps on a rebuilding roster.

## Esmerlyn Valdez, OF, Pittsburgh Pirates

Valdez signed for $130,000 out of the Dominican Republic in 2021 and has been hitting the ball harder every year since. He slugged 22 <span class="term" data-term="home run">home runs</span> at Low-A in 2024 despite a 30.6 percent strikeout rate and a steep swing that scouts called "flimsy." Then he made a mechanical adjustment. Eric Longenhagen at FanGraphs identified the change: his hands now set up more vertically, and he cut down his <span class="term" data-term="attack angle">attack angle</span> and chase rates. The result was 26 home runs across High-A and Double-A in 2025, Arizona Fall League (AFL) Offensive Player of the Year, a Futures Game appearance, and a strikeout rate that dropped to 24.6 percent. FanGraphs rates his raw power at 60/65, approaching 70 grade according to Baseball America's Statcast analysis. Longenhagen comps him to Randal Grichuk.

The scorecard sees why. His Barrel Rate ranks third in the entire pool, and he's pairing it with strong discipline numbers at both the swinging strike rate and chase rate. The concern is his <span class="term" data-term="consistency gap">Consistency Gap</span>, the widest in his tier, which means the barrel production comes in bursts rather than as a steady baseline. That's the "all-or-nothing" signature that scouts describe, and it's the thing that separates a potential 25-homer bat from a guy who runs hot and cold. Defensively, he's limited (fringy defender in right field or at first base), but Pittsburgh's outfield isn't locked down.

**Verdict:** Stash in all formats with NA spots. The power is legitimate, the discipline has improved at every level, and there's a confirmed mechanical change driving it. Longenhagen projects him contributing to Pittsburgh's outfield mix by late 2026. The Grichuk comp sets realistic expectations: a corner bat who hits for power with some swing-and-miss, not a batting average asset.

## The Watch List

**Hector Rodriguez (CIN, OF, 22)** is maybe the most interesting name here relative to his scouting profile. FanGraphs describes him as "one of the most aggressive, chase-prone hitters in pro baseball during the last half decade," with chase rates "multiple standard deviations worse than the major league average." Despite that, he's never hit below .280 in a pro season because his bat control is that good. Every scouting report identifies the chase rate as the one thing separating him from being an everyday player.

The scorecard shows that metric dropping 18.3 points year-over-year, the largest single-metric discipline improvement in the entire data set. The Reds added him to the 40-man roster after 2025, and Baseball America says he could make Cincinnati this year. If the chase rate improvement is even half real, the projection changes.

**Bryce Eldridge (SFG, 1B/DH, 21)** is a different caliber of prospect than anyone else on this list. FanGraphs ranks him 16th overall with a 55 <span class="term" data-term="future value">Future Value</span> grade and 70/80 raw power grades. He signed for nearly $4 million out of high school in 2023, hit 25 home runs across Double-A and Triple-A in 2025, and debuted in September with a three-run double off Tyler Glasnow. He played 2025 through a bone spur in his left wrist that required surgery in October, which likely explains the elevated strikeout rate. The scorecard has him in Tier C with a Luck Index suggesting his results are worse than his contact quality. At 21, that's a foundation, not a red flag. Casey Schmitt is holding down first base with a .739 OPS through April 12. Eldridge should be back soon.

**James Tibbs III (LAD, OF, 23)** is the hottest hitter in the minors. I expected a guy mashing like this to show up higher than Tier C, and I want to be honest about what's underneath.

The Giants drafted him 13th overall out of Florida State in 2024, then dealt him to Boston in the Devers trade, and the Red Sox flipped him to the Dodgers for Dustin May six weeks later. Three organizations in two months.

The Dodgers adjusted his swing mechanics (leg kick became a timing step), and he's been mashing since. His Barrel Rate is the second-highest in the pool, and the power is real. But the Luck Index is the most negative of any qualifying prospect, meaning his results are running well ahead of his contact quality. Both FanGraphs and Baseball America describe a player who can be beaten with premium velocity. The scorecard puts him in Tier C. The heater will cool, and the path to playing time in LA (behind Tucker, Hernandez, Pages, and Ohtani) is crowded. Stash if you have the roster space, but manage your expectations.

## What the Scorecard Is Telling Us

What connects Watson, Antonacci, and Valdez isn't that they're all hitting well. It's that they're all improving in the metrics that stabilize first. Chase rate, swinging strike rate, and barrel rate all reach meaningful signal by 50 PA, and all three of these guys are showing movement in at least two of those categories at once. You don't have to wait for the box score to tell you a player has changed if you know where to look.

The number to watch across the board is chase rate at 120 PA. That's roughly where discipline improvements either hold or wash out. Watson's at 57, Antonacci's at 53, Rodriguez is at a similar count. If those chase rates are still where they are now in six weeks, the scouting reports will need updating.
