There is a hitter in Major League Baseball who is currently batting .542 this spring. His OPS is 1.647. In 53 plate appearances, he has seven home runs, a rate that projects over a full season to somewhere north of 85. He is 26 years old, plays for the Cincinnati Reds, and is currently going in the fourteenth round of most fantasy drafts.

He is not going to hit .542.

The <span class="term" data-term="batting average">batting average</span> is the loudest number in his spring line, which means it is the least useful one. Before we use any number in this player's spring line for anything, we need to establish which ones are telling the truth and which are performing.

Two numbers are. They are not the ones most people are looking at.

## Spring Batting Average

In March, major league rosters are open competitions. Regulars share the lineup with players who will spend the year in Triple-A. Pitchers are building arm strength rather than executing game plans. The matchups are scrambled in ways they never are during the season, and they stay that way for six weeks.

This is why <span class="term" data-term="spring training">spring training</span> batting average is noise. It measures results in an environment where the results depend heavily on who happens to be pitching, and who happens to be pitching in March has almost no relationship to who will be pitching in June. A hitter can bat .400 in spring against a mix of regulars and mop-up relievers and go on to hit .230 for the season. The sample is small, the competition is uneven, and the outcomes are unreliable.

Our hitter's BABIP, batting average on balls in play, the measure of how often his batted balls become hits rather than outs, is .543. The major league average BABIP is around .300. A mark of .543 means roughly half the balls he has put in play this spring have found open grass. That rate will not hold. With infielders positioned correctly and outfielders at full attention, a significant portion of those hits become outs.

Spring training is the only period in the calendar when most casual fans are actively watching games with a specific evaluative purpose. The regular season produces 162 games of information; most of it gets processed in aggregate, in statistics, weeks after the at-bats happen. Spring training produces a handful of games per team, each one nationally streamed, with draft-eligible players in the lineup and millions of fantasy players watching. A hot week in March reaches more people who are paying attention than a hot week in July ever does. That is not a reason to trust the hot week. It is a reason to understand why the market overweights it.

## The Numbers That Hold

There are spring training numbers that carry signal. They share a common trait: they reflect how a hitter is swinging, not what happens to the ball once it leaves the bat.

<span class="term" data-term="strikeout rate">Strikeout rate</span> doesn't depend on where fielders are standing or what the BABIP happens to be. A hitter either makes contact or he doesn't. He either identifies a ball out of the zone or he chases it. These outcomes are determined by the hitter alone, which means they hold up even in the competitive mess of March. <span class="term" data-term="walk rate">Walk rate</span> carries the same logic.

Batted ball shape carries it too. When a hitter puts the ball in play, the initial direction — ground ball, fly ball, <span class="term" data-term="line drive">line drive</span> — is set at contact. The geometry of the swing determines whether the bat drives through the center of the ball on a slight upward path, sending it forward at a useful angle, or catches it underneath, sending it into the dirt. That geometry doesn't change based on who's pitching. <span class="term" data-term="ground ball rate">Ground ball rate</span> and line drive rate are mechanical fingerprints, and mechanical fingerprints don't wash off against Triple-A competition.

This is what you should be reading in a spring line. Not the average. Not the home run count.

## Matt McLain's 2025 Numbers

His name is Matt McLain.

In 2025, McLain played 147 games for Cincinnati, his first full major league season. He batted .220. His wRC+, a park- and league-adjusted measure of offensive value where 100 is average, was 77. Below average, by a margin that showed up in his draft price: heading into 2026, his <span class="term" data-term="adp">ADP</span> sat at 163.6, deep in the fourteenth round, a player the market had mostly filed away.

The underlying contact data wasn't the problem. His <span class="term" data-term="exit velocity">exit velocity</span> was 88.8 miles per hour, functional if not elite. His <span class="term" data-term="hard-hit rate">hard-hit rate</span> was 40.6 percent. His <span class="term" data-term="barrel rate">barrel rate</span> was 7.7 percent. These are the numbers of a hitter with something to work with.

What suppressed his production was specific: a strikeout rate of 28.9 percent and a line drive rate of 17.2 percent. He was missing too much, and when he made contact, the ball went on the ground more often than it should. His ground ball rate was 38.7 percent; the typical power hitter tends to be closer to 35, and McLain wasn't generating enough power from his contact to make the ground balls forgivable.

At the plate, McLain looks like what his swing data says he is: controlled, quiet, the compact approach of a middle infielder who has been coached to stay within himself. His <span class="term" data-term="attack angle">attack angle</span> in 2025 was 6.9 degrees, below the 10 to 15 degrees that tend to generate consistent lift. Not as extreme as some of the position's flatter swingers, but not steep enough to give the ball reliable air. The result: too many hard grounders dying before the outfield grass.

His <span class="term" data-term="expected slugging">expected slugging</span> was .362 against an actual slugging of .343. The gap was small. He wasn't dramatically unlucky. He just wasn't hitting the ball the right way often enough.

In spring training, two of those numbers moved.

His strikeout rate fell to 11.3 percent. His line drive rate climbed to 30.95 percent. His walk rate stayed essentially flat at 9.4 percent, meaning the strikeout drop wasn't because he stopped swinging. It's because he started making more contact when he did.

Those three numbers, together, describe a different hitter than the 2025 box score showed. A 17-point drop in strikeout rate, paired with a near-doubling of line drive rate and a walk rate that held steady, suggests contact that is more frequent and better-directed. His ground ball rate fell from 38.7 percent to 30.95 percent. When a hitter makes more contact and that contact is sharper and better-angled, the production usually follows.

## What Won't Hold

The seven home runs won't hold. McLain's home run to fly ball rate this spring is 43.75 percent. The major league average is around 13 percent; the best power hitters in the game sustain 20 to 25 percent over a full season. The home runs will regress toward something closer to what the <span class="term" data-term="projection systems">projection systems</span> expect: BATX projects 17.8 home runs over a full season, OOPSY has him at 20. Both of those numbers were built on his 2025 performance. Neither has had time to incorporate whatever he has changed this spring.

The projection systems are working from the right baseline. They're just not working from the right swing.

There is one further complication worth naming. McLain's <span class="term" data-term="bat speed">bat speed</span> in 2025 was 69.7 miles per hour. Freddie Freeman, at 36, was at 69.9. McLain is 26. Low bat speed in a young player is unusual, and it suggests his power ceiling may be more limited than his spring home run total implies. The contact efficiency improvement — making more contact, hitting more line drives — can produce real value in batting average and runs. A 30-home-run season would require the bat speed to rise along with the contact rate, and there is no spring bat tracking data yet to confirm it.

The honest read: the contact improvements may be real. The power ceiling has a constraint the spring doesn't clearly resolve.

At 163.6 ADP, the market is pricing McLain as though the 2025 box score is the whole story. The K% and line drive rate changes are the argument that it isn't. Projection systems anchor on results; the spring batted ball data anchors on mechanics. When those two things tell different stories, the mechanical numbers tend to be the more honest forecast.

The number to watch in April is not his batting average. It is his strikeout rate. In 2025, it was 28.9 percent. This spring, it is 11.3 percent. The truth in 2026 is probably somewhere between those two numbers — a change this dramatic across 53 plate appearances will partially regress. But if McLain carries even half of that improvement into the regular season, the 2025 box score stops being the right reference point.

That gap between 28.9 and 11.3 is where his 2026 season lives.
