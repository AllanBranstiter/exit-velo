28.9 percent. Then 11.3 percent. Both numbers belong to Matt McLain, who plays shortstop for the Cincinnati Reds and is currently going in the fourteenth round of most fantasy drafts. The first is his strikeout rate from 2025. The second is his strikeout rate from the last three weeks of March.

The question isn't whether he's been good this spring. He has. His <span class="term" data-term="ops">OPS</span> is 1.647, he has seven home runs in 53 plate appearances, and he is batting .542. The question is which number to believe.

The <span class="term" data-term="batting average">batting average</span> is the wrong one.

## The .543 BABIP

McLain's 2025 season ended with a .220 average and a <span class="term" data-term="wrc+">wRC+</span> of 77, below average by enough that the fantasy market mostly stopped thinking about him. His <span class="term" data-term="adp">ADP</span> heading into 2026 sits at 163.6, deep in the fourteenth round, priced as a speculative add rather than a draft asset.

Before 2024, he had shown what the ceiling looks like. In 89 games in 2023, his debut season, McLain hit .290 with a 129 wRC+ and 16 home runs. A labrum injury ended his spring training in 2024 and cost him the entire season. His 2025 numbers read less like a ceiling than a first full season back from surgery.

His .542 AVG looks like a correction. It isn't, exactly. His <span class="term" data-term="babip">BABIP</span> this spring is .543. The major league average over a full season is around .300, meaning roughly three in ten balls in play fall for hits. For McLain, it's been just over half this spring.

Either way, his AVG and BABIP will normalize. The infielders in March are not always the infielders who will be there in June, and the pitchers throwing to stretch out at Goodyear are not the pitchers who'll be throwing max effort to win games in September.

## K% and Batted Ball Shape

There are <span class="term" data-term="spring training">spring training</span> numbers that carry signal. They share one quality: they measure what a hitter does with the swing, not where the ball lands afterward.

<span class="term" data-term="strikeout rate">Strikeout rate</span> is one of them. A hitter either makes contact or he doesn't. He identifies a pitch out of the zone or he chases it. These are outcomes the hitter controls, which means they hold up in July as much as March. <span class="term" data-term="walk rate">Walk rate</span> carries the same logic.

Batted ball shape carries it too. The angle of the swing at contact determines whether the ball climbs or dies: a bat that drives through the center of the ball on a slight upward path sends it into the air; one that skims underneath sends it into the dirt. That outcome is set before the pitcher matters, before the defense matters. <span class="term" data-term="ground ball rate">Ground ball rate</span> and <span class="term" data-term="line drive">line drive</span> rate are mechanical fingerprints. They tend to operate the same way regardless of who's pitching.

In 2025, McLain's strikeout rate was 28.9 percent and his line drive rate was 17.2 percent. He was missing too often, and when he made contact, the ball went on the ground. His ground ball rate was 38.7 percent. His <span class="term" data-term="attack angle">attack angle</span> was 6.9 degrees, below the 10 to 15 degree range that tends to produce consistent lift. At the plate he looks like what the numbers say: controlled, compact, the quiet approach of a middle infielder coached to stay within himself. The result was a lot of hard grounders dying before the outfield grass.

McLain was making good contact. His <span class="term" data-term="exit velocity">exit velocity</span> was 88.8 miles per hour, his <span class="term" data-term="hard-hit rate">hard-hit rate</span> was 40.6 percent, and his <span class="term" data-term="barrel rate">barrel rate</span> was 7.7 percent. These are the numbers of a hitter who can hurt you. The contact quality wasn't the problem. His K% and batted ball shape were.

This spring, two of those numbers moved. McLain's strikeout rate fell to 11.3 percent. His line drive rate climbed to 30.95 percent. His walk rate stayed flat at 9.4 percent, which matters: the strikeout drop didn't come from taking more pitches. It came from making more contact when he swings.

His ground ball rate fell from 38.7 percent to 30.95 percent. **A 17-point drop in strikeout rate paired with a near-doubling of line drive rate describes a different approach, not a different talent level.**

## The Bat Speed Problem

This spring's seven home runs won't hold. McLain's <span class="term" data-term="hr/fb">home run to fly ball rate</span> this spring is 43.75 percent. The major league average hovers around 13 percent; the best power hitters sustain 20 to 25 percent over a full season. The home runs will regress toward what the <span class="term" data-term="projection systems">projection systems</span> expect: Steamer projects 15 home runs for the season, and OOPSY has him at 20. Both were built from his 2025 performance and haven't had time to incorporate whatever he has changed.

There is one constraint worth naming. McLain's <span class="term" data-term="bat speed">bat speed</span> in 2025 was 69.7 miles per hour. That's comparable to Freddie Freeman, who was at 69.9. Freeman is 36. McLain is 26. Low bat speed in a young hitter is unusual, and it suggests his power ceiling may be more limited than the spring home run total implies.

He was also still recovering in 2025 from a serious labrum injury the previous year. Players often need a full season after that kind of surgery, and his bat speed numbers may reflect a shoulder that wasn't quite right. His barrel rate in 2023, before the surgery, was 10.8 percent. In 2025, it was 7.7 percent. Whether the shoulder has improved this spring is the one thing the available data doesn't resolve.

The contact improvements may be real. But getting to 30 home runs would require the bat speed to rise along with the contact rate, and there is no spring bat tracking data yet to confirm it has.

At 163.6 ADP, the market is pricing McLain as though the 2025 box score is the whole story. The strikeout rate and line drive rate changes are the argument that it isn't. Projection systems anchor on results. Spring batted ball data anchors on mechanics. When those two things point in different directions, the mechanical numbers tend to be more predictive.

**The number to watch during the first weeks of the regular season is not his batting average, it's his strikeout rate.** In 2025, that number was 28.9 percent. This spring, it is 11.3 percent. A change that dramatic across 53 plate appearances will partially regress. But if McLain carries even half of that improvement into the regular season, the 2025 season stops being the right reference point.

That gap is where his 2026 season lives.
