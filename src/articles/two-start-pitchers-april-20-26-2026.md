Twenty-one pitchers rostered in fewer than half of CBS leagues have two starts scheduled for the week of April 20-26. The fantasy industry treats this as a volume opportunity, a chance to stack innings and strikeouts from the wire. And I get the appeal. Two starts means two chances at a <span class="term" data-term="quality start">quality start</span>, two chances at a win, double the counting stats for free.

The problem is that most of these pitchers will hurt you. NFBC data from 2022 showed that pitchers used as one-to-two-game streamers posted a collective 5.23 <span class="term" data-term="era">ERA</span>. The managers who finished at the top of the standings didn't win by streaming. They won by hoarding starters with 100-plus innings and avoiding the shiny names on the two-start list.

**The edge in a two-start week isn't finding pitchers with two starts. It's finding the ones whose talent indicators say the results are real, and ignoring the ones whose April ERAs are running on fumes.** I went through the pitch-level data for every sub-50-percent-rostered pitcher on next week's list, pulled their <span class="term" data-term="stuff+">Stuff+</span>, <span class="term" data-term="pitching+">Pitching+</span>, and <span class="term" data-term="location+">Location+</span> model grades, compared their 2025 and 2026 arsenals pitch by pitch, checked the <span class="term" data-term="run value">run values</span> and <span class="term" data-term="whiff%">whiff rates</span> on every offering, and ran each profile through the evaluation framework from *The Process 2026*. Three pitchers came out looking like genuine adds. A handful more are streamable in the right matchup. And several of the prettiest ERAs on the board are built on sand.

FIP, or fielding independent pitching, estimates what a pitcher's ERA should be based only on the outcomes he controls: strikeouts, walks, hit-by-pitches, and home runs. It strips out the noise of balls landing in fielders' gloves or runners getting stranded by coincidence. In April, when every pitcher has fewer than 20 innings, FIP is a far better indicator of true talent than ERA. And the Pitching+ column (Pit+) is a model-based grade from <span class="term" data-term="baseball savant">Baseball Savant</span> that evaluates the physical quality and location of every pitch a pitcher throws, independent of results. A score of 100 is league average. It stabilizes almost immediately because it grades the pitches themselves, not what happened after hitters swung at them.

Those two numbers are the spine of this piece. When ERA and FIP agree, you can trust the results. When they don't, something is lying. And when Pitching+ confirms which version is right, you can act before the rest of your league figures it out.

## The Adds

**Sean Burke, RHP, Chicago White Sox (14% rostered) — @ARI, vs WAS**

Burke is the best add on this list, and the reason is subtraction. He threw five pitches in 2025: a four-seam fastball, a <span class="term" data-term="slider">slider</span>, a <span class="term" data-term="curveball">curveball</span>, a <span class="term" data-term="changeup">changeup</span>, and a <span class="term" data-term="sinker">sinker</span>. The changeup was terrible, a minus-4.5 run value per 100 pitches with a .474 <span class="term" data-term="xwoba">xwOBA</span> against, which means hitters were crushing it nearly every time they put it in play. The sinker was worse in a quieter way, generating zero strikeouts and a .385 xwOBA across 115 pitches. In August, Chicago sent him to Triple-A.

He came back a different pitcher. In 2026, Burke has dropped both the changeup and the sinker entirely, simplifying to a three-pitch mix. And the pitches he kept all got better. His fastball Stuff+ jumped from 101.5 to 115.5, which is a massive leap for a single offseason. Its run value flipped from -0.2 to +1.0, its xwOBA against dropped from .311 to .260, and it's generating a 40 percent strikeout rate in 104 pitches. His slider flipped from a liability (minus-1.3 run value) to a weapon (+2.2), with the <span class="term" data-term="whiff%">whiff rate</span> climbing from 26.4 to 30.4 percent.

*The Process 2026*, the fantasy evaluation framework by Jeff Zimmerman and Tanner Bell, has a line I keep coming back to: "Dropping a bad pitch is sometimes more valuable than adding a good one." Burke did both. The <span class="term" data-term="location+">Location+</span> model confirms the improvement is about command, not just stuff: every remaining pitch grades above average in location, and the composite Location+ jumped from 100.5 to 111.1. His <span class="term" data-term="walk rate (pitcher)">walk rate</span> dropped from 10.6 percent in 2025 to 4.8 percent, which aligns with everything the pitch models are showing. His FIP is 1.96. His ERA is 3.60, mostly because his strand rate has been abnormally low (58.8 percent, against a league average of about 72), meaning runners have scored at an unlucky clip despite the improved pitching. That gap should close in his direction.

His most recent start (April 16 against Tampa Bay) wasn't great, and that's probably why he's still available. Fantasy managers check the last game log before making a claim. This is a case where the last game log is the least useful data point. **Start both.**

**Spencer Arrighetti, RHP, Houston Astros (53% rostered) — @CLE, vs NYY**

Arrighetti's absence from the early-season data files confused me until I found the March 23 transaction: Houston optioned him to Triple-A when Lance McCullers won the fifth rotation spot. He was recalled on April 15 and immediately struck out 10 against Colorado on April 16, hitting a couple of batters along the way, which is a pretty concise summary of the Arrighetti experience. Electric arm, questionable command, never boring.

I don't have 2026 FanGraphs data for him yet because he wasn't on the MLB roster when the files were last refreshed. What I do have is a 2025 baseline (102 Stuff+, 97 Pitching+, a 12.8 percent walk rate that is genuinely alarming), a Razzball projection that values his next two starts at $19.8 (the highest among any pitcher near the 50 percent threshold), and a 10-strikeout outing that suggests the stuff hasn't gone anywhere.

The case for adding him is simple: his strikeout upside is real, the Astros trust him enough to recall him into the rotation, and his roster rate is about to spike after the 10-K game. The case against is equally simple: the walks. A 12.8 percent walk rate in 2025 is a serious flaw. He hit two batters on April 16, which isn't a walk but it's adjacent. If the command tightens, he's a hold. If it doesn't, he's a streamer with a high ceiling and a basement floor. Either way, **add him now before the 10-K headline does its work.**

**J.T. Ginn, RHP, Athletics (3% rostered) — @SEA, @TEX**

Ginn is rostered in 3 percent of leagues and he's pitching next week in the two most pitcher-friendly parks in baseball. T-Mobile Park in Seattle has an overall <span class="term" data-term="park factor">park factor</span> of 91, meaning it suppresses run-scoring by about 9 percent relative to a neutral environment. Globe Life Field in Texas sits at 92. These aren't marginal edges. These are the extreme environments in MLB.

But the park matchups aren't the reason to add him. The talent is. His <span class="term" data-term="sinker">sinker</span> grades at 142.0 Pitching+, which is elite for any pitch in baseball. Its Stuff+ improved from 108.7 to 116.7 year over year, and the <span class="term" data-term="baseball savant">Savant</span> results confirm the model: sinker run value went from +0.4 to +4.3, xwOBA against dropped from .313 to .254. His slider took a step forward too, with Stuff+ climbing from 90.2 to 104.3. The overall Pitching+ composite of 114.6 is the second highest among all the pitchers I evaluated for this piece.

His 2025 season across 90.1 innings showed a 25.3 percent strikeout rate and a 7.9 percent walk rate, which is a 17.3 percent K-BB%, the kind of efficiency that supports a mid-rotation starter. He's at the center of Oakland's 26-inning scoreless streak, per MLB.com, and the A's have been trusting him with increasingly important assignments. The one concern: his 2026 K rate has dipped to 19 percent in 11 innings, and a recent start featured walks. But 11 innings is too few to read a K-rate decline as meaningful, and his swinging strike rate (10.4 percent) is consistent with his 2025 profile.

I'd add him in any league with 12 or more teams and start both with confidence. **The parks are the bonus. The sinker is the argument.**

## The Streams

**Max Meyer, RHP, Miami (53% rostered) — vs STL, @SF**

Meyer is the steadiest profile on this list. His Pitching+ composite (106.1) has barely moved from 2025, his sinker grades well (117.9 Pit+), and his changeup flipped to become his best pitch by run value (+3.4). The fastball is still a liability (.408 xwOBA against), but his second start is at <span class="term" data-term="park factor">Oracle Park</span> in San Francisco, where the HR factor is 77. That's a 23 percent suppression of home runs relative to neutral, the most extreme HR-suppression park in the majors. For a pitcher whose fastball gets hit hard but rarely barreled, Oracle is the ideal environment. Start both, and feel especially good about the San Francisco start.

**Keider Montero, RHP, Detroit (13% rostered) — vs MIL, @CIN**

Montero's improvement story is about command, not stuff. His Stuff+ actually declined slightly (96.7 to 92.7), but his Location+ jumped from 101.0 to 109.2. Every pitch he throws now grades above average in location, and his K-BB% went from 10.5 percent to 21.1 percent in 10.1 innings, which is a dramatic shift if it holds. The problem is the second start: Great American Ball Park in Cincinnati has a 119 HR park factor, meaning home runs are inflated by nearly 20 percent. For a pitcher who hasn't allowed a homer yet in 38 batters faced and is statistically due for some, that's the wrong park at the wrong time. Stream vs MIL at Comerica Park. Sit @CIN.

**Rhett Lowder, RHP, Cincinnati (49% rostered) — @TB, vs DET**

Lowder is a location-dependent pitcher with 81.6 Stuff+ and 112.6 Location+, which means he has below-average raw pitch quality but above-average command. His <span class="term" data-term="k% (pitcher)">strikeout rate</span> is 15.9 percent, well below average, which caps his upside in formats that reward punchouts. He has no 2025 MLB baseline. His first start is at Tropicana Field (park factor 96), which is fine. His second is at home at Great American, and the same HR inflation concern that applies to Montero applies here. Stream the Tampa Bay start if you need innings.

## The Traps

This is the section where the article earns its keep. If all I had to offer was "add the good pitchers and avoid the bad ones," you wouldn't need me. The interesting part is what happens when the box score says one thing and the pitch-level data says the opposite.

**Michael McGreevy, RHP, St. Louis (35% rostered) — @MIA, vs SEA**

Sports Illustrated ran a headline last week calling McGreevy "St. Louis' next star pitcher." His ERA is 2.49 in 21.2 innings. The fantasy industry is bullish.

I dug into his pitch-level data and found something less flattering. His Stuff+ composite is 75.8, which is the lowest among every pitcher I evaluated for this piece. And it's not one bad pitch dragging him down. His Stuff+ declined on every single pitch type from 2025. His fastball Stuff+ is 70.4, the lowest individual pitch grade in this entire analysis, and the xwOBA against that fastball is .498, meaning hitters are absolutely teeing off on it when they make contact. His whiff rate on the pitch dropped from 23.8 to 11.4 percent.

How is the ERA so low? Location and luck. His Location+ is 113.8, with every pitch grading above average in command. He's putting the ball exactly where he wants it. But his <span class="term" data-term="babip (pitcher)">BABIP</span> is .188 (league average is around .300), and his strand rate is 87 percent (league average is about 72). Those two numbers mean that balls in play are finding gloves at a historically unusual rate and runners who reach base aren't scoring. That won't continue. When the BABIP normalizes, those fastballs that hitters are squaring up with .498 expected quality are going to start finding gaps.

He also dropped his sweeper and curveball from the 2026 mix, which together made up 32 percent of his 2025 arsenal. That's a significant reduction in pitch diversity. His changeup stepped into the void (usage more than doubled to 22.1 percent) and it's been his best pitch (+4.3 run value), but one good secondary pitch doesn't fix the fundamental problem: below-average stuff that hitters can and will hit hard once the sequencing luck runs out. **Avoid both starts.**

**Justin Wrobleski, LHP, Los Angeles Dodgers (34% rostered) — @COL, vs CHC**

Wrobleski just threw eight shutout innings against the Mets on April 14. Multiple outlets reported he made "major changes" to his approach, and the Dodgers beat writers described a more aggressive, simplified attack. The fantasy industry is treating him as a breakout.

The pitch-level data tells a more complicated story. In 2025, his fastball had a 35.2 percent whiff rate and a 44.9 percent strikeout rate across 312 pitches. It was a genuine swing-and-miss weapon. In 2026, that same fastball has an 8.6 percent whiff rate and a 3.7 percent strikeout rate across 112 pitches. He's throwing it nearly half the time now (49.1 percent usage, up from 28.6), and hitters are putting it in play almost every time they swing. His Stuff+ on the pitch dropped from 106.6 to 92.5.

He simplified from six pitches to essentially two, the fastball and slider making up 86 percent of his arsenal. The slider is still working (+4.6 run value, 25.6 percent whiff rate), but a pitcher can't survive on one pitch when the primary offering isn't missing bats. The 2.12 ERA is being held up by a .154 BABIP, the lowest on the entire two-start list, which means that the balls hitters are putting in play against him are finding gloves at a rate that simply will not hold.

And his first start is at Coors Field. **Avoid both, but especially avoid the Coors start.** A pitcher who isn't missing bats at the league's most extreme hitter's park (park factor 112) is asking for trouble.

## The Number to Watch

Burke's walk rate. In 2025, it was 10.6 percent across 134.1 innings. In 2026, it's 4.8 percent. The difference between those two numbers is the difference between a streamer and a hold. The pitch-level data (Location+ jump from 100.5 to 111.1, arsenal simplification, dropped two pitches that were dragging down his command) all point to the improvement being structural. At 30 innings, if the walk rate is still below 6 percent, the improvement is real and Burke is a roster hold for the rest of the season. If it's drifted back toward 9 or 10 percent, the 2025 version is the baseline and the two-start add was still fine, just not a long-term solution. I think the pitch data says it's real.
