<p>Twenty-one pitchers rostered in fewer than half of CBS leagues have two starts next week. Most of them will hurt your ratios. The three worth adding are Sean Burke (CHW), Spencer Arrighetti (HOU), and J.T. Ginn (ATH), all of whom show real pitch-level improvement backed by <span class="term" data-term="pitching+">Pitching+</span> and <span class="term" data-term="stuff+">Stuff+</span> model grades, not just low <span class="term" data-term="era">ERAs</span>. The biggest traps on the board are Michael McGreevy (2.49 ERA, 75.8 Stuff+, .188 <span class="term" data-term="babip (pitcher)">BABIP</span>), Justin Wrobleski (2.12 ERA but a 9.5 percent <span class="term" data-term="k% (pitcher)">strikeout rate</span>), and Chad Patrick (0.73 ERA with a 100 percent strand rate). All three have April ERAs propped up by luck indicators that will not hold.</p>

<p style="margin-top:1rem;"><strong>If you're new to baseball:</strong> When a pitcher's ERA (the number of runs he allows per nine innings) is very low but the quality of contact against him is average or worse, it usually means he's been lucky rather than dominant. Balls that were hit hard happened to land in gloves, or runners who reached base happened not to score. Analysts use metrics like Stuff+ (how good a pitcher's pitches are based on their physical properties) and FIP (a version of ERA that strips out the luck) to separate skill from fortune.</p>

<p style="margin-top:1rem;"><strong>If you manage a fantasy team:</strong> Add Sean Burke (14% rostered) in all formats and start both his starts. He dropped two bad pitches over the offseason, his fastball <span class="term" data-term="stuff+">Stuff+</span> jumped 14 points, and his <span class="term" data-term="walk rate (pitcher)">walk rate</span> halved. Add Spencer Arrighetti (53% rostered) before his roster rate spikes after a 10-strikeout outing on April 16. Add J.T. Ginn (3% rostered) for two starts in the two most pitcher-friendly <span class="term" data-term="park factor">parks</span> in baseball. Drop or bench McGreevy, Wrobleski, and Patrick regardless of their ERAs.</p>

Twenty-one pitchers rostered in fewer than half of CBS leagues have two starts scheduled for the week of April 20-26. Two starts means two chances at a <span class="term" data-term="quality start">quality start</span>, two chances at a win, double the counting stats for free. **The problem is that most of these pitchers on this week's list will hurt you.**

**That said, let's try to find the pitchers whose talent indicators say their early 2026 results are real, and callout the ones whose April ERAs are running on fumes.** I went through the pitch-level data for every sub-50-percent-rostered pitcher on next week's list, pulled their <span class="term" data-term="stuff+">Stuff+</span>, <span class="term" data-term="pitching+">Pitching+</span>, and <span class="term" data-term="location+">Location+</span> model grades, and compared their 2025 and 2026 arsenals pitch by pitch. I checked the <span class="term" data-term="run value">run values</span> and <span class="term" data-term="whiff%">whiff rates</span> on every offering. Three pitchers came out looking like genuine adds. A handful more are streamable in the right matchup. And several of the prettiest ERAs on the board are built on sand.

**FIP, or fielding independent pitching, estimates what a pitcher's ERA should be based only on the outcomes he controls: strikeouts, walks, hit-by-pitches, and home runs. It strips out the noise of balls landing in fielders' gloves or runners getting stranded by coincidence. In April, when every pitcher has fewer than 20 innings, FIP is a far better indicator of true talent than ERA.

The K/9 column (strikeouts per nine innings) tells you whether the pitcher is actually missing bats, which is the single most predictive and stable skill a pitcher can have in a small sample.

<span class="term" data-term="pitching+">Pitching+</span>, a model-based grade from <span class="term" data-term="baseball savant">Baseball Savant</span> that evaluates the physical quality and location of every pitch independent of results, is referenced throughout the player write-ups below. A score of 100 is league average. It stabilizes almost immediately because it grades the pitches themselves, not what happened after hitters swung at them.

Those numbers are the spine of this piece. When ERA and FIP agree, you can trust the results. When they don't, something is lying. And when K/9 and Pitching+ confirm which version is right, you can act before the rest of your league figures it out.

One more tool that shows up in the player write-ups: <span class="term" data-term="pitch velocity">velocity trends</span>. *The Process* estimates that every 1 mph a starter loses costs roughly 0.28 ERA. In a group of 21 pitchers with fewer than 25 innings apiece, year-over-year velocity change is one of the most stable signals available, and several of the pitchers on this list have moved meaningfully in either direction.

Burke is the best add on this list, and the reason is subtraction. He threw five pitches in 2025: a <span class="term" data-term="four-seam fastball">four-seam fastball</span>, a <span class="term" data-term="slider">slider</span>, a <span class="term" data-term="curveball">curveball</span>, a <span class="term" data-term="changeup">changeup</span>, and a <span class="term" data-term="sinker">sinker</span>. The sinker was worse in a quieter way, generating zero strikeouts and a .385 xwOBA across 115 pitches. In August, Chicago sent him to Triple-A.

He came back a different pitcher. In 2026, Burke has dropped both the changeup and the sinker entirely, simplifying to a three-pitch mix. And the pitches he kept all got better. His fastball Stuff+ jumped from 101.5 to 115.5, which is a massive leap for a single offseason. Its run value flipped from -0.2 to +1.0, its xwOBA against dropped from .311 to .260, and it's generating a 40 percent strikeout rate in 104 pitches. His slider flipped from a liability (minus-1.3 run value) to a weapon (+2.2), with the <span class="term" data-term="whiff%">whiff rate</span> climbing from 26.4 to 30.4 percent.

*The Process*, the fantasy evaluation framework by Jeff Zimmerman and Tanner Bell, has a line I keep coming back to: "Dropping a bad pitch is sometimes more valuable than adding a good one." Burke did both. The <span class="term" data-term="location+">Location+</span> model confirms the improvement is about command, not just stuff: every remaining pitch grades above average in location, and the composite Location+ jumped from 100.5 to 111.1. His <span class="term" data-term="walk rate (pitcher)">walk rate</span> dropped from 10.6 percent in 2025 to roughly 6 percent in 2026, which aligns with everything the pitch models are showing. Through 20.1 innings, his FIP is 3.81 and his ERA is 4.43, with a rough April 16 outing against Tampa Bay inflating both numbers. Before that start, the FIP was 1.96 in 15 innings.

*The Process* recommends blending early-season data at 33 percent current, 67 percent prior year for the best predictive accuracy. That gives Burke a blended K-BB% of about 13 percent, which falls in the adequate range rather than the elite range the raw 2026 numbers suggest. But the direction of every indicator, the stuff improvement, the command improvement, the deliberate pitch-trimming, all point the same way. His fastball velocity has ticked down slightly over three years (95.3 in 2024, 94.4 in 2025, 94.0 in 2026), which is worth monitoring but not yet alarming at 94 mph.

Razzball's per-start projection for his next outing (@ARI) is modest: 5.0 innings, 4.4 strikeouts, 4.50 ERA. That projection likely leans on his 2025 rates, which is exactly the version of Burke I'm arguing no longer exists. Projection systems are slow to recognize arsenal changes, and this is a case where the pitch-level data is ahead of the models.

His most recent start (April 16 against Tampa Bay) wasn't great, and that's probably why he's still available. Fantasy managers check the last game log before making a claim. This is a case where the last game log is the least useful data point. **Start both.**

## The Number to Watch

Burke's walk rate. In 2025, it was 10.6 percent across 134.1 innings. In 2026, it's 4.8 percent. The difference between those two numbers is the difference between a streamer and a hold. The pitch-level data (Location+ jump from 100.5 to 111.1, arsenal simplification, dropped two pitches that were dragging down his command) all point to the improvement being structural. But 15 innings isn't proof.

At 30 innings, if the walk rate is still below 6 percent, the improvement is real and Burke is a roster hold for the rest of the season. If it's drifted back toward 9 or 10 percent, the 2025 version is the baseline and the two-start add was still fine, just not a long-term solution. I think the pitch data says it's real. I'd be happy to be proven wrong, because that would mean I got to watch a 26-year-old pitcher figure himself out in real time. Either way, the number to watch is walks.
